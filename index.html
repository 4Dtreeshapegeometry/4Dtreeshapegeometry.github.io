A Riemannian Framework for the Elastic Analysis of the Spatiotemporal Variability in the Shape and Structure of Tree-like 4D Objects

Tahmina Khanam1, Hamid Laga1, Mohammed Bennamoun2, Guanjin Wang1, Ferdous Sohel1, Farid Boussaid2, Anuj Srivastava 3
1Murdoch University, 2University of Western Australia 3Florida State University
Paper Supplementary Code

Abstract

This paper presents a novel computational framework for modelling and analyzing the spatiotemporal shape variability in tree-like 4D objects, i.e., 3D objects whose shape deforms over time. Tree-like 3D objects, such as botanical trees and plants, deform and grow at different rates. In this process, they bend and stretch their branches and change their branching structure, making their spatiotemporal registration challenging. We address this problem using a Riemannian approach that treats tree-like 3D objects as points in a tree-shape space equipped with a proper metric that quantifies branch bending and stretching as well as topological changes. A 4D tree-like object can then be seen as a trajectory in this space. Thus, the problem of modelling and analyzing the spatiotemporal variability in tree-like 4D objects becomes that of analyzing trajectories embedded in this space. However, performing the spatiotemporal registration and subsequently computing geodesics and statistics on the tree-shape space, which is nonlinear, is not straightforward, as these tasks rely on complex nonlinear optimizations. Our core contribution is the mapping of the tree-like 3D objects to the space of the Extended Square Root Velocity Field (ESRVF) where the complex elastic metric is reduced to the L2 metric. By solving the spatial registration in the ESRVF space, the problem of analyzing tree-like 4D objects becomes that of analyzing elastic trajectories embedded in the ESRVF space. In this paper, we develop the building blocks that enable such analysis. These include: (1) the spatiotemporal registration of tree-like 4D objects in the presence of large elastic shape deformations, large topological differences, and large variations in their growth rates; (2) the computation of geodesics between tree-like 4D objects; (3) the computation of statistical summaries such as means and modes of variation; and (4) the synthesis of random tree-like 4D objects. We demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed framework using simulated 4D botanical tree models as well as real plants.
